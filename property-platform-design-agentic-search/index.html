Before I show you anything, I want you to picture someone. Her name is Sarah. She's 32. She got mortgage pre-approval last week, and right now it's Sunday afternoon in Mt Eden. She's on the couch with her phone in one hand and a flat white in the other. She wants a three-bedroom place she can grow into. She wants it near a decent primary, because she and her partner have a 4 year-old boy. She doesn't want to commute more than an half an hour. Her cap is $900K.

Sarah is not in my deck. She's in your App Store reviews. She's in your saved-search logs. And today, she's the reason I'm here.

I'm Mari Kondrateva. Ten-plus years of product design — mobile-first, user-centered, conversion-focused, fintech and SaaS. The last year hands-on with AI agents at Galvanized Pukeko. I want to tell you Sarah's story twice. Once on MyDoorof today. Once on the MyDoorof Guide. The difference between those two stories is the design challenge.

Screen 1 — Onboarding

Sarah opens MyDoor. Same orange. Same tabs. Same listings. New surface — a chat box in the focus, filters one tap away below it. The Guide isn't a new product. It's a new layer on the existing one. The visual language is yours, lifted directly from your current welcome screen.

Screen 2 — Ask, turn one

Sarah types her sentence. "Buy 3-bed near a good primary, around 30 min from the Auckland CBD, under $900." Her query becomes a chat bubble — the pattern she already knows from ChatGPT, from WhatsApp, from every conversation app on her phone. According to NZ stats, about 79% of New Zealand adults already use conversational AI. We aren't training her on a new mental model.

The Guide streams its working steps inline. Understanding your needs. Searching 46,000 listings. Checking school zones — source: Ministry of Education. Calculating commute times — source: Maps. Cross-referencing suburb medians — source: CoreLogic. Each step carries a data-source pill. Two purposes. One — it builds trust, because Sarah can see exactly what's being used and where it came from. Two — while she's reading, the specialist is working in parallel. Transparency masks latency. Two birds.

Property cards arrive in the thread. Match percentages — 94%, 89% — and a short Why this matches panel under each. Three bed, walkable to Maungawhau School, decile 9. 47-minute commute. Listed 8% under suburb median. Every claim sourced. Nothing invented.

Screen 3 — Follow-up, turn two

This is the moment chat earns its place over a list view. Sarah types three words. "Walk time to a primary?" The Guide doesn't search again. It re-ranks the existing shortlist in place, using conversation state. That's the signature capability — multi-turn refinement without losing context. A list view can't do this. A filter can't do this. This is what justifies chat over a smart-filter variant — and yes, I considered the smart-filter variant first. I rejected it because the market has moved past it, and because spending the budget there means underspending on the agents and the eval harness, which is where the actual quality comes from.

Screen 4 — Compare, turn three

The Guide proactively suggests "Want me to compare your top three?" Sarah taps yes. The Guide returns a structured side-by-side card — bed, bath, school, commute, price-against-median, urgency signals — and a plain-English verdict bubble underneath. "On lifestyle and school zone, A is strongest. On price and value, B is the better long-term play. C has the most uncertainty in suburb trend." It doesn't decide for her. It frames the decision. Suggests, never decides — that's the rule that makes everything else permissible.

Screen 5 — Listing detail

Sarah taps into A. Familiar MyDoor listing surface — photos, price, agent. New element below — a Guide Insights card. In zone for Maungawhau Primary, decile 9. 47 minutes to your partner's office at peak. 8% under Mt Eden's three-year median. Two open homes booked this weekend — high agent activity. Each line is sourced. Nothing invented. This is the Guide showing up at the moment of decision with the data it's already gathered, summarised in a glance.

Screen 6 — For You

Tuesday morning. Sarah's having a coffee break at work. Her phone in hand. "A new property matches your shortlist — same school zone, same budget." That's Learn mode. Proactive, not push-notification carpet bombing. Below it, a card asking if she'd like the Guide to start learning her preferences over time — opt-in, never on by default. Earned autonomy. We don't get to be proactive until we've earned the right.

That's Sarah's new Sunday. And her new Tuesday. The journey: ask, refine, evaluate, compare, return.
