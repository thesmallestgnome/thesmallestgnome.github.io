Mark wants to sell his place in Albany. Right now in MyDoor that means ~80 form fields and a blank Description textarea between him and a live listing. He's sat on his couch for an hour and got as far as "Property type."

Mark is not in your competitor's pitch deck. He's in your churn cohort. He starts a listing, runs into the form, and goes back to ringing an agent instead. Today, he's the reason I'm here.

Same Guide character from the buyer side — same plum brand, same chat surface, same data-source pills — now applied to the listing flow. One address field instead of eighty.

Screen 1 — Welcome

Mark opens MyDoor for Sellers. The Guide leads with one ask: paste your address. Underneath are two quieter escape hatches — snap a rates bill, or talk it through — and a "skip the Guide" link for the power users who'd rather fill the long form themselves. We're not removing the form, we're demoting it.

Screen 2 — Fact fetch, turn one

Mark sends his address. The Guide streams its working steps inline. Matching LINZ title. Land area from the LINZ survey. Rateable value from Auckland Council. Year built and floor area from the council's building consent record. Suburb median from CoreLogic. School zones from the Ministry of Education. Each step carries a data-source pill. Same transparency pattern as the buyer-side prototype, same two birds — trust on one hand, latency-masking on the other. Mark reads while the specialist works.

Five seconds in, the Guide reports back. "I've got 23 of the 27 facts the listing needs. There are 4 things only you can tell me." ~80 form fields just collapsed to four questions. Above the input there's a row of quick-reply chips — Review the facts, What's still missing, Edit the address — so Mark doesn't have to know what to type next.

Screen 3 — Review, turn two

The Guide returns the form pre-filled — as a chat bubble, not a new page. Every confirmed line carries its source pill: LINZ for the title and land area, Auckland Council for the rateable value, Council BCO for the build data. Nothing invented.

The four rows in butter yellow are tagged "You" — material chip, beds and baths steppers, sale-type chip. Two taps each. This is the payoff of the public-record pre-fill: the seller only answers what the council and the registry can't.

The chat input is still at the bottom of the screen. Mark can break out of the form at any moment — "Why this rateable value?" — and the Guide picks the conversation up from there. The form is inside the chat, not the other way round.

Screen 4 — Photos & draft

Mark taps the camera button next to the mic, drops six photos. The Guide drafts the listing description from them and the facts already confirmed. The highlighted spans are the sourced claims — 3,198 m², 2008, weatherboard, 168 m², north-facing (detected in photos 2 & 4). The description is the one section a seller has to write from scratch, and the one section that actually sells the property. The Guide hands Mark a starting point he can edit, not a blank page.

Underneath: a coverage check. Two ticks (bedrooms, bathrooms, aspect & sun) and two flags with reasoning. School zone not mentioned — 73% of buyers searching Albany filter by school zone. Albany Primary & Albany Senior High are in-zone. Each flag has an "+ Add" button so Mark can accept the suggestion in one tap. Suggests, never decides — same rule as the buyer side.

Screen 5 — Buyer preview

The closing move: Mark sees his listing the way Sarah will see it. Hero image, price, address, specs — and a Guide Insights card that mirrors the buyer side's transparency, summarised in a glance. 91% match for buyers like Sarah. In zone for Albany Primary and Albany Senior High — source: Ministry of Education. Priced near suburb median ($1.29M) — sits in 89% of Albany buyers' budgets — source: CoreLogic. Large section (3,198 m²) — top quartile for Albany — source: LINZ.

Closes the loop with the buyer-side prototype. Same Guide, same sourced claims, same character on both sides of the marketplace. Mark hits publish.
